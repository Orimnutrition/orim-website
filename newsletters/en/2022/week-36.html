The weekly letter on preventive immunonutrition

WEEK

36

September 5-11, 2022

EDITORIAL

Selenium is a trace mineral with outsized importance for thyroid function, antioxidant defense, and immune competence. This week, we explore why selenium status matters and how regional soil variations create invisible deficiency risks even in well-nourished populations.

01

SMARTFARMING & AGRITECH

Selenium and Thyroid Hormone Activation

The thyroid gland contains more selenium per gram than any other organ. Selenoproteins including deiodinases are essential for converting inactive T4 to active T3 thyroid hormone. A 2021 meta-analysis in Thyroid journal demonstrated that selenium supplementation (200mcg/day) reduced thyroid peroxidase antibodies by 40% in Hashimoto's thyroiditis patients, suggesting a direct immunomodulatory role in autoimmune thyroid disease.

Glutathione Peroxidase and Selenium

Selenium is the catalytic center of glutathione peroxidase (GPx), the body's primary enzymatic antioxidant defense system. GPx neutralizes hydrogen peroxide and lipid hydroperoxides that would otherwise damage cell membranes and DNA. A 2021 study in Free Radical Biology showed that selenium intake below 55mcg/day reduced GPx activity by 50%, dramatically increasing oxidative vulnerability across all tissues.

European Selenium Deficiency

European soils are significantly selenium-depleted compared to North American soils, resulting in lower selenium content in regionally grown cereals, vegetables, and animal products. A 2022 Lancet Planetary Health analysis mapped selenium intake across Europe, finding that Swiss, French, and German populations averaged 40-60mcg daily versus the 70mcg recommended by EFSA. Brazil nuts are the most concentrated dietary source.

02

BIOLOGICAL ADVANCES

Brazil Nut (Bertholletia excelsa)

A single Brazil nut delivers 70-90mcg of selenium, meeting the entire daily requirement. The Amazonian trees that produce these nuts have roots extending 50 meters deep, mining selenium from ancient geological deposits unavailable to shallow-rooted crops. This makes Brazil nuts a remarkably efficient selenium source. However, exceeding 3-4 nuts daily risks selenosis, as the therapeutic window for selenium is narrow.

Eat exactly 2 Brazil nuts daily for optimal selenium intake. More is not better: chronic intake above 400mcg/day causes selenosis symptoms including hair loss, nail brittleness, and garlic breath from dimethyl selenide.

THIS WEEK IN BRIEF

► THYROID: Hashimoto's thyroiditis prevalence reaches 5% of the European female population, with selenium deficiency identified as a modifiable risk factor in recent endocrinology guidelines.

► SOIL: Finnish government continues mandatory selenium fertilizer program begun in 1984, maintaining population selenium status through soil enrichment rather than supplementation.

► RESEARCH: Oxford-Harvard joint study finds inverse association between selenium status and COVID-19 severity, adding to growing evidence of selenium's role in viral immune defense.
