ORIM WEEKLY

The weekly letter on preventive immunonutrition

WEEK

June 3-9

EDITORIAL

June opens with abundant produce and peak daylight hours. This week we explore the gut-brain-immune triangle and how early summer nutrition supports cognitive function alongside immunity. Fermented dairy takes the spotlight as a dual-action food for microbiome and mineral delivery.

01

SMARTFARMING & AGRITECH

Gut-Brain-Immune Axis: Psychobiotics

Specific probiotic strains termed 'psychobiotics' modulate the gut-brain axis via vagal nerve signalling and tryptophan metabolism. A 2023 meta-analysis in Molecular Psychiatry showed that Lactobacillus helveticus R0052 and Bifidobacterium longum R0175 reduced anxiety scores by 30% while simultaneously increasing salivary IgA by 20%. Mental and immune health share microbial mediators.

Summer Solstice Melatonin Patterns

The June solstice compresses melatonin secretion to a 5-6 hour window (compared to 8-9 hours in winter). A 2024 study in the Journal of Pineal Research showed that this compressed pattern increases melatonin amplitude by 15%, concentrating its immune effects into a shorter but more intense nocturnal window. Maintaining consistent bedtime maximises this benefit.

Silvopasture and Enhanced Milk Nutrient Profiles

Cattle grazed in silvopasture systems (trees integrated with pasture) produce milk with 40% higher conjugated linoleic acid and 25% more fat-soluble vitamins than confinement-raised cattle. A 2023 study in the Journal of Dairy Science attributed this to diverse browse intake and reduced heat stress. Agroforestry dairy farming produces immunologically superior milk products.

02

BIOLOGICAL ADVANCES

Kefir: Living Probiotic Matrix

Traditional kefir contains 30-50 different bacterial and yeast species, far exceeding any commercial probiotic supplement. Its unique kefiran exopolysaccharide has been shown to modulate dendritic cell maturation, bridging innate and adaptive immunity. The fermentation process also increases B-vitamin content by 100% and pre-digests lactose, making it accessible to 90% of lactose-intolerant individuals.

Best: Make kefir at home with authentic grains. Commercial kefir typically contains only 10-12 species versus 30-50 in traditional preparations.
